What is Malware?

  • Malware, or malicious software, is defined as a code with malicious intent that typically steals data or destroys the files/documents on the computer.
  • Malware can be installed to your devices by just clicking on the malicious links, or by downloading malicious attachments or third-party software, or by connecting to unsecured wi-fi which gets misused by cybercriminals.
  • Once your device is infected, it can spy on the activities you perform on your devices or it may steal, delete or make the data unusable.

One Of The Most Damaging Types Of Malware Is Ransomware

  • Ransomware is a form of malware that encrypts a victim's files. The cybercriminal then demand a ransom from the victim to restore access to the data upon payment.

More On Different Types Of Malware

  • Virus - By attaching to clean files, once downloaded, viruses can infect other files and then spread uncontrollably, damaging the core functionality of systems by deleting or corrupting files.
  • Trojan (or Trojan Horse) - By disguising itself as a legitimate software, once inside a network, a Trojan can set up backdoor entries to allow other types of malware to enter the network.
  • Worm - It infects the entire network or device by replicating rapidly. As it spreads, a worm overloads infected systems and may introduce other malware.
  • Spyware - Targeted at a person or an organization, spyware aims to steal personal or device information which is then shared without consent.
  • Keylogger (or Keystroke logging) - By secretly recording the keys struck on a keyboard, a cyber criminal can harvest sensitive information like passwords and logins.
  • Malvertising - Malvertising or malicious advertising, uses what looks like legitimate online advertising to distribute malware and other threats with little to no user interaction require
  • Malicious Browser Extension - These can be installed into a browser through malicious app store downloads masquerading as legitimate extensions throug social engineering or by an adversary that has already compromised a system. Once installed, the extension can browse to websites in the background and steal all information entered into the browser including login credentials.

What Is Social Engineering?

Have you looked into Physical Security?

  • Cyber criminals may use physical means to steal sensitive data or install malware. Popular techniques include:
  • Tailgating (or piggybacking) involves closely following an authorized person into a restricted access are As an employee opens the door to enter a restricted area, the tailgater may grab at the door handle just as it is about to close, hence gaining access into that restricted area.
  • Baiting is like the real-world ‘Trojan Horse’ in that it relies on the curiosity or greed of the victim. With the end goal of infiltrating a company’s network, the cyber criminal may leave malware-infected flash drives or similar devices lying around or distribute it in a conference as a ‘free gift’, in hopes that this hardware will be inserted into network-connected computers, thereby spreading the malicious code.
  • Dumpster Diving is the act of diving into the trash of a business or an individual with the aim of finding valuable information among the discarded items, such as sticky notes written with access codes and passwords and other paper documents.

Are Your Passwords Secure?

  • Passwords are the keys to access sensitive data. A majority of hacking incidents are due to stolen or weak passwords. Cybercriminals commonly use hybrid methods to steal/crack passwords to access your personal or organization's data. Below are some common password attack methods:

Brute force attack

The hacker uses a computer program to login to the victim's account with all possible password combinations. Depending on the complexity and length of the password, it can take from a few seconds to years to crack the passwor

Dictionary attack

The hacker tries to guess a password by trying random words that can be found in a dictionary. They'll often make small changes to those words by adding symbols and /or numbers until they find a match.

Credential stuffing

A login attack whereby hackers use the organization's stolen login credentials, often purchased from the dark web of shared on online forums, to try to access accounts within the organization until they hit a match

Password spraying attack

Also called the 'low-and-slow' method, the cyber criminal uses a single commonly used password, such as Password123, to attempt to gain access to all the accounts on their list.

What is a Drive-By-Download Attack?

  • In Drive-By-Download (DBD) attacks, the victim does not need to comply with instructions from a hacker or download any malicious codes.
  • In DBD, malware or viruses can be planted in one or all of the following phases:

Phase 1: The hacker first seeks out a popular and legitimate website with high traffic He/she then looks for flaws in the website’s codes, security protocols or outdated programming languages and injects an exploit kit (ie malicious pieces of software created by hackers to identify vulnerabilities in a device, web browser, or web-based app).

Phase 2: Victims can pick up the malware through downloads by simply visiting the compromised website. This can also be triggered by seemingly unobtrusive interactions with a pop-up. For instance, by clicking on the close button on a pop-up ad, a malicious code may be downloaded.

Phase 3: Without antivirus / anti-malware, the DBD will automatically install itself into the victim’s device as a Drive-By Install (DBI) thereby successfully creating a point of entry to freely hijack the victim’s device, spy on activities, corrupt and/or destroy the device’s internal storage to cause damage to valuable data

What is Phishing?

  • Phishing is a cyber crime that relies on deception to steal confidential information from users and organizations.
  • This is done via downloading of malicious attachments which in turn install hacking tools/malware on laptops or by incfluencing the victims to give up their confidential information.
  • The victims may be tricked into replying to a fraudulent message that offers lucrative deals, promotions or rewards, or demands urgent action. These messages can come in the form of emails, text messages, social media, voice mail, scam calls, etc.

Common Phishing Tactics

  • Phishing tactics usually involve one of the following actions by the victim:
  • Click on a link
  • Open malicious attachments and/or enabling macros
  • Scan QR codes to receive freebies
  • Update their passwords
  • Respond to a social media request
  • Access a new Wi-Fi hotspot
  • Install new softwares/apps
  • Complete urgent payment requests

Phishing - What Cyber Criminals Look For?

  • Cyber criminals will typically ask for:
  • Date of birth
  • NRIC/FIN
  • Singpass Pin
  • Phone numbers
  • Bank or credit card details
  • Home address
  • Password information (or information to reset password)
  • One-time passwords (OTP)

Phishing Consequences!

  • If sensitive information is collected on a large scale, cyber criminals may start a more targeted cyber attack such as spear phishing or business email compromise; these attacks rely on knowing more about the victim(s).
  • By using phishing emails to install malware on the victim's device, the infected device can be used to conduct more cyber attacks e.g, malicious emails sent from the infected device to colleagues or friends to infect their devices as well.
  • Cyber criminals may also use the information collected to access victims' bank accounts or trade the information on the Dark Web.

Types Of Phishing

Deceptive Phishing/Impersonation

  • This is the most common type of phishing
  • Cyber criminals will impersonate a legitimate company to steal personal data or login credentials.
  • They may also employ threats and urgency language in emails to scare users into compliance.

Spear Phishing

  • A target attack on the victim.
  • Cyber criminals will customize emails with the target's name, position, company, and other information to trick the recipient into believing that they have an authentic connection with the sender.

CEO Fraud or Whaling

  • Executives are usually targeted.
  • Cyber criminals spoof a CEO or other high ranking executives to authorize fraudulent financial transfers.
  • They also use information on employees (PII) for other targeted attacks.

Email Hijacking

  • This is another form of a man-in-the-middle attack.
  • The cyber criminal compromises and gains accesss to the email account of Party A, and monitors hte email communications between two parties; i.g., Party A and Party B.
  • The attacker then uses the existing email thread to send a malicious email to Party B.
  • The recipient, Party B, is tricked into believing that he is interacting Party A and may comply with the fraudulent request.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

  • In vishing, the cyber criminal contacts the victim via phone or voice call and uses either IVR (Interactive Voice Response) or mimics known entitles to steal sensitive data and/or funds.

SMShising(SMS Phishing)

  • Victims are contacted via SMS/text messages. The cyber criminal mimics known entitiles to steal sensitive data and/or funds.

Quishing (QR Code Phishing)

  • With the adoption of online payment transactions and vouvher registerations, malicious QR codes may be embedded to lead unsuspecting vistims to fake websites, thereby tricking them into providing their personal information.
  • When victims enter their personal data on the fake website, cyber criminals can use that information to take control of the victims' various accounts (including their digital wallets)
  • They may also employ threats and urgency language in emails to scare users into compliance.
